DRI: Justin Zhou  •  Promo window: April 1–30, 2026  •  Data as of Apr 28, 2026
Verdict
The incentive moved adoption slightly, but did not move durability.

Treatment drove a statistically significant +3.5% relative lift in phone bill payment rate (+376 incremental payers). However, durability was flat — no meaningful difference between treatment and control. Most of the $125K payout went to users who would have paid their phone bill anyway.

8 Interpretation

What happened

The 20% cashback offer produced a small, statistically significant lift in phone bill adoption (+376 incremental payers, +3.5% relative). But this lift did not translate into higher durability or measurably higher engagement. The experiment moved the behavior slightly but not the outcome we care about.

Why the cost is high

The fundamental problem is deadweight spend. Of the 12,462 treatment users who paid a phone bill, ~12,086 would have done so anyway (matching the control). The $125K payout rewards existing behavior in 97% of cases. The 376 truly incremental payers cost ~$333 each — not viable at scale.

What this means for H2

The original hypothesis was: “Does paying a bill on Crimson cause high engagement behavior?” This experiment does not support a causal link between phone bill payment and durability. The +0.7% relative lift in durability is not statistically significant (p ≈ 0.62). Bill payers are more engaged, but paying a bill does not appear to make users more engaged.

Still pending

H3 (stickiness) and H4 (cross-bill adoption) require May data. The Week 8 readout (May 29) will show whether treatment payers retained their phone bill in May without an incentive, and whether they adopted other bill types. These results could change the recommendation if the behavior persists.

Not ROI-positive and no significant shift in durability. Retire the current bonus structure. Pending May persistence data (H3/H4) which could move this to “One-Time Incentive” if stickiness is strong.
