8 Interpretation

What happened

The 20% cashback offer produced a small, statistically significant lift in phone bill adoption (+376 incremental payers, +3.5% relative). But this lift did not translate into higher durability or measurably higher engagement. The experiment moved the behavior slightly but not the outcome we care about.

Why the cost is high

The fundamental problem is deadweight spend. Of the 12,462 treatment users who paid a phone bill, ~12,086 would have done so anyway (matching the control). The $125K payout rewards existing behavior in 97% of cases. The 376 truly incremental payers cost ~$333 each — not viable at scale.

What this means for H2

The original hypothesis was: “Does paying a bill on Crimson cause high engagement behavior?” This experiment does not support a causal link between phone bill payment and durability. The +0.7% relative lift in durability is not statistically significant (p ≈ 0.62). Bill payers are more engaged, but paying a bill does not appear to make users more engaged.

Still pending

H3 (stickiness) and H4 (cross-bill adoption) require May data. The Week 8 readout (May 29) will show whether treatment payers retained their phone bill in May without an incentive, and whether they adopted other bill types. These results could change the recommendation if the behavior persists.

7 Interpretation

What this cohort tells us

When you remove the ~42K dashers who were already paying their phone bill on Crimson before April, the picture sharpens. The full-cohort 3.5% adoption lift was diluted by these users (who would have paid regardless). On the cohort the bonus was actually designed to activate, the lift is +9.5% relative — a much stronger signal.

But durability still doesn’t move

Even on this cleaner cohort, durability is statistically flat (+0.07 pp, p ≈ 0.28). Ancillary deposits, POS spend, and dashing hours are all unchanged. Phone bill payment alone doesn’t cause engagement in low-engagement users within a single month.

The cost story improves but doesn’t pay off

Targeting non-historical-payers brings cost-per-incremental-payer from $333 to $118 (a 64% improvement) and cost-per-incremental-durable-user from $3,908 to $392. That last number is closer to the $210 target from the experiment design but still ~1.9× over. Even the “best” cohort doesn’t hit ROI within the promo month.

Still pending: May persistence

The strongest case for this incentive would be if treatment new adopters keep paying phone bills in May (no incentive) and accumulate engagement over time. The Week 8 readout (May 29) will tell us whether the 276 incremental payers stick — that’s the only path to a positive ROI given current cost structure.
